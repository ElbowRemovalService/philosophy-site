A Brief Defense of Altruism

Altruism has rather famously been attacked by Ayn Rand, founder of Objectivism (a philosophy I tend to refer to as Randianism or Randian Objectivism to avoid confusion with metaphysical and epistemological objectivism). To establish her case against altruism, we must look to her own words.

What is the moral code of altruism? The basic principle of altruism is that man has no right to exist for his own sake, that service to others is the only justification of his existence, and that self-sacrifice is his highest moral duty, virtue and value.

Thus, we have her first argument: that altruism dictates that the existence of man is only justified if that man serves others.

Do not confuse altruism with kindness, good will or respect for the rights of others. These are not primaries, but consequences, which, in fact, altruism makes impossible. The irreducible primary of altruism, the basic absolute, is self-sacrifice—which means; self-immolation, self-abnegation, self-denial, self-destruction—which means: the self as a standard of evil, the selfless as a standard of the good.

Thus, the second argument: that altruism, being an ideology in which man sacrifices for others, cannot allow kindness or respect, and is necessarily a self-destructive ideology.

Do not hide behind such superficialities as whether you should or should not give a dime to a beggar. That is not the issue. The issue is whether you do or do not have the right to exist without giving him that dime.

We see hear the crux of Rand's critique: she argues that, as the issue is “whether you do or do not have the right to exist without giving him that dime,” that altruism necessitates that your own right to existence depends upon giving the hypothetical beggar the hypothetical dime.

To give a final quote from Rand:
Altruism declares that any action taken for the benefit of others is good, and any action taken for one's own benefit is evil. Thus the beneficiary of an action is the only criterion of moral value—and so long as that beneficiary is anybody other than oneself, anything goes.

We see that Rand has declared that altruism is an ideology which states that only actions which benefit others can be good, and any action which benefits yourself is evil.

Having now established several of Rand's criticisms, we shall now examine them.
Firstly, we inspect the claim that under altruism, man has no right to exist if he does not act to assist others. Unfortunately for Rand, here we find that she has been attacking a strawman. An infant child cannot exist without being helped - nor can our hypothetical beggar survive without his hypothetical dime - and many other examples. Would altruism claim that our beggar is evil for having received aid, or our infant immoral for having been raised? Such a claim is eminently ridiculous. A philosophy built upon acting to benefit others necessitates that others must be willing to accept actions which benefit them. Our beggar, then, has acted entirely in his own self-interest by accepting the coin. Altruism, then, requires that some act in their own self-interest at least some of the time. The claim, then, that altruism requires total destruction of the self is self-evidently false.
Further analyzing Rand's claim about altruism's requirements on existence, we discover that altruism, which according to Oxford may be defined as “the belief in or practice of disinterested and selfless concern for the well-being of others,” does not state that one ought to exist only if they help others. It is self-evident that man exists, and in altruism, we do not find a claim that man should exist only while assisting others; instead, we find the claim that to assist others is a moral good. Additionally, we do not find the claim that the only moral good is helping others, only that to help another is a moral good. As the title suggests, I am a proponent of altruism as I understand it. One could well argue that what I propose is not altruism, but a variant. I would agree that perhaps my understanding of altruism is separate from others, but I do not think myself departed from the umbrella of altruism.

Now, we must examine Rand's second claim: that altruism is necessarily self-destructive and cannot allow for kindness nor respect. Altruism would be self-destructive if it dictated that all action must necessarily be both in the self-interest of others and exclusionary to the self-interest of one's self. This is eminently not the case. As Rand herself argued (correctly), it is quite possible to benefit others by acting in your own self-interest, as our capitalistic system, when not interfered with, does so. By equal measure, it is then possible to do the inverse, and benefit one's self by acting to benefit others. However, as stated previously, altruism does not dictate that the only acceptable action is one which benefits another; neither does it state that any action which benefits oneself is not acceptable. Altruism is then not systematically and necessarily self-destructive. This is not to say that it cannot be self-destructive; one could very easily give everything they have and thus ensure their own homeless demise (assuming none would assist them as they assisted others, of course, and further assuming that they would not foolishly give away that which they have just been given in similar vein), but by no means is this a necessary aspect of altruism. It is apparent that one cannot help others if one cannot help themselves. It is incoherent, then, for a proponent of altruism to claim that altruism necessitates total self-sacrifice, and equally incoherent for an enemy of altruism to declare it so.
The failures of Rand's critique of altruism (or perhaps more fairly, altruism as I understand and defend it) seem to boil down to a critique of the claim that “the only good is acting against your own self-interest and in the interest of another” rather than attacking the claim that “to act in concern for the well-being of another is a moral good.” However, even Rand herself admitted later in her life that it was not evil to help another. To return to our hypothetical beggar, Randians must answer the question: is it evil to give the dime to the beggar? If the answer is no, it must be posited; why? If our followup is answered in some variety of “Because you are acting in your self-interest,” the Randian must give up any and all critique of voluntary altruism. Returning to the original question, if the answer is yes, it is evil to give the dime to the beggar, once again, our followup is why? If the answer is “Because that is an action for the well-being of another,” could not then this criticism be used against capitalism, or even just trade in general? It would seem that the critique of altruism boils down to a claim that because one does not receive materialistic compensation for one's actions that one's actions are evil.

To address a final statement of Rand's on altruism, as all previous ones are, in my opinion, satisfactorily addressed, we turn to Rand's statement on “forced altruism.” She stated that communism was enforced altruism, and used this as an argument against altruism. This statement, of all her critiques, is, in my opinion, the most fallacious. Altruism is necessarily voluntary. If one is forced to give up their substance for another's benefit, they have not chosen to act out of a concern for another's well-being. If one has been robbed, this does not make one an altruist. Altruism does not dictate that each man has a right to the substance of those more fortunate, only that it is good for the more fortunate to use their substance to help the less fortunate, presuming of course that no other principle would make this action immoral. Altruism necessitates that the individual has the freedom to act, and further requires property rights in order for one to use their own substance for another's benefit. In order for one to consistently support altruism, they must also support individualism and property rights. In conclusion, Rand's arguments against altruism fall completely flat. The entity which she calls altruism is surely contradictory, but altruism it is not.
