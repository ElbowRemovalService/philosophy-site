As an immediate point of clarification, knowledge is defined philosophically as true justified belief. Thus, statements about knowledge are also to some degree statements about what is true, as most philosophers were more interested in that modifier than in the justification or the belief. We must choose from empiricism, rationalism, relativism, and skepticism. In order to know which is right, we shall first employ a process of elimination. Let us examine each of our four primary schools of thought and their variations.

Skepticism

Skepticism is the position which states that some or all knowledge is unknowable. Firstly, let us examine extreme or total skepticism. Total skepticism would state that all knowledge is unknowable, although the truth may exist. This position is immediately contradictory, as the knowledge that all knowledge is unknowable is knowledge that, if the position were true, ought to be unknowable. Thus is revealed a performative contradiction, or an internal contradiction that must be true and false at once. However, as the law of noncontradiction must be true (A = A. B = B. A =/= B. Thus, A cannot = B), the claim is necessarily false. The total skeptic may backpedal and say that all other knowledge is unknowable, but they can make no distinction as to why that knowledge is itself knowable, for not only can no reason exist, but were they to provide one, that reason would encompass knowledge, which should be unknowable. This process continues until the total skeptic must admit defeat, being forced to take instead the position that only some knowledge is unknowable. This position, however, fails to distinguish them from any other position, as no rationalist nor empiricist will argue the claim that man cannot know everything, for man is not omniscient. Skepticism, then, is clearly not a position worth taking.

Relativism

Relativism would state that knowledge or the truth is relative, or variable between person to person or culture to culture. Extreme relativism would state that all aspects of reality are variable to the perception of an individual. This position would hold that, were I to decide that 2 + 2 = 5, it would suddenly become so. This position is obviously false, for were it true, any individual could believe that this position is false. This would require relativism to be both true and false, violating the law of noncontradiction. Individual relativism, then, must necessarily fall short. Cultural relativism would state that what is true is dependent from culture to culture. This stance typically only arrives in moral spheres. This stance has the same flaw as before. If any culture decides relativism is false, relativism breaks the law of noncontradiction. An additional flaw, however, becomes apparent. What is a culture? What is a society? If a culture is merely a majority of people, in what sphere do we denote a majority? A majority of all people living? Of all that have lived? Of all living in a certain area? How do we denote to which area counts? Is an island with three people a culture? If two of the three decide to murder the third, is that morally acceptable because they are the culture? Thus we see that relativism fails on all counts.

Empiricism

Empiricism would claim that knowledge comes from observation and experimentation alone. This claim also seems to fall flat when it is considered that if reason does not determine knowledge, than how can observing something allow one to conclude anything? In reality, it is inductive reasoning which allows observation to have any power. As it turns out, there have been no statistically valid studies (peer-reviewed, blinded, and controlled) proving that parachutes work to prevent people from falling to their death. It is empirically invalid to claim, then, that parachutes function. However, this is patently ridiculous, as skydiving is quite clearly something done and survived. Thus, empiricism is lacking, if not totally decrepit like the previous two entries into this list. Empiricism would qualify as part of the philosophy of objectivism (lower case "o"), which states that the world is objective and real, and not subject to human thinking as to how the laws of nature functions.

Rationalism

Rationalism remains the final option, and, in my opinion, the best option. Rationalism is also the most common sense option. Rationalism would claim that knowledge is acquired by the mind using reason to determine fact. This can occur after observation or experimentation as well as from pure reason itself. For example, an a priori (pure reason) rationalistic claim would be one that "One cannot argue that one cannot argue." If someone argues that they can argue that they can't argue, they are arguing, and thus engaged in a performative contradiction. Hence, the statement about argumentation is objectively true by nature of it being impossible for it to be false. Thus, rationalism alone fully passes our internal tests. Rationalism is also objectivist, and arguably more so than empiricism.
