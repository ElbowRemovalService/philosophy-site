Praxeology: The Study of Action

Ludwig von Mises, Founder of Praxeology
Diogenes, an infamously irrational man

Praxeology, a term coined by the German economist Ludwig von Mises, is the study of human (or intelligent) action. Praxeology is the foundation of economics (the study of human exchange and interaction) as well as a large part of morality (how man should act). Praxeology can be rather simply explained.

  • Man acts. This principle is irrefutable, for to say that man doesn't act, you have acted.
  • Man has preference. Certain things are liked more or less than others. This is subjectively evaluated. I may prefer apples to oranges, and you may prefer oranges to apples.
  • Man acts with purpose. All of man's actions have a goal in mind. These actions are designed to realize his preference. This does not mean his actions are a rational method of realizing preference.
  • Inaction is a form of action. If you choose not to decide, you have still made a choice.

Morality is the primary concern of man's action in most people's minds. Firstly, it is considered what morality is. There are three primary positions: First, that morality does not exist. As morality is merely how man should act (and it is a given that man will act), this stance can be immediately discarded, for we know that man has preference, and man's actions have differing outcomes. The second position is that morality is subjective, differing from either person to person or culture to culture. This stance falls for the same error as subjective or relative epistemology (as outlined in Epistemology). Thus, it may be discounted. Finally, we reach the third primary position: that morality is objective. This must be our stance. As for what is moral and what isn't is a much longer discussion.
